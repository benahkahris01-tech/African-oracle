EPS
Earnings Per Share

EPS tells you how much profit a company made for each share that exists. It is the single most important number for understanding whether a company is actually making money.

EPS = Net Profit ÷ Number of Shares Outstanding
Real example — Safaricom (SCOM)

Safaricom earned KES 69.8 billion in net profit in FY2025. With 40 billion shares outstanding, that works out to an EPS of approximately KES 1.74. This means every share of Safaricom "earned" KES 1.74 of profit for its owner.

What to look for: Growing EPS year over year is a strong positive sign. A company whose EPS went from KES 5 to KES 12 over 5 years is growing its earnings powerfully. Negative or declining EPS is a red flag.
On The African Oracle, we show Beginning EPS (5 years ago) and End EPS (most recent) so you can see the full journey at a glance.
P/E
Price-to-Earnings Ratio

The P/E ratio tells you how many years of current earnings you are paying for when you buy a share. Think of it as the price tag relative to the profits.

P/E = Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share
Real example — Equity Group (EQTY)

If Equity Group trades at KES 69 and its EPS is KES 12.33, then P/E = 69 ÷ 12.33 = 5.6. This means you are paying 5.6 years' worth of earnings for each share — very cheap by most global standards.

What to look for: There is no universally "correct" P/E. African markets often trade at lower P/E than US or European markets because of perceived risk. A P/E below 10 on NSE or JSE can signal genuine value. A P/E above 30 means the market expects very strong future growth.
On The African Oracle, P/E under 10 appears in green, 10–20 is neutral, above 20 is amber — colour-coded to help you spot cheap vs expensive at a glance.

PEG
Price/Earnings-to-Growth Ratio

PEG improves on the P/E ratio by factoring in the company's growth rate. A fast-growing company deserves a higher P/E than a slow one — PEG accounts for this.

PEG = P/E Ratio ÷ EPS Growth Rate (%)
Real example — KCB Group (KCB)

KCB has a P/E of about 4 and its 5-year EPS growth rate is roughly 30%. PEG = 4 ÷ 30 = 0.13. A PEG below 1 is generally considered undervalued. At 0.13, KCB appears significantly undervalued relative to its growth.

Rule of thumb: PEG below 1.0 = potentially undervalued. PEG around 1.0 = fairly valued. PEG above 2.0 = potentially expensive. Popularised by legendary investor Peter Lynch.
PEG under 1 shows in green on The African Oracle. It is one of our buy signal inputs.
Div Yield
Dividend Yield

Dividend yield tells you what percentage of your investment you receive back each year as cash dividends. It is the "income" part of investing in stocks, separate from price gains.

Dividend Yield = Annual DPS ÷ Current Share Price × 100
Real example — Safaricom (SCOM)

Safaricom paid KES 1.20 per share as dividend in FY2024. At a share price of KES 27.50, the dividend yield = 1.20 ÷ 27.50 = 4.4%. This means if you owned KES 100,000 of Safaricom shares, you would receive KES 4,400 in cash dividends per year.

What to look for: A yield above 5% on an NSE stock is generally attractive for income investors. However, an unusually high yield can sometimes signal a falling share price rather than a generous company — always check if the dividend is sustainable from earnings.
NSE yields are calculated from hardcoded annual DPS data. JSE yields use the same method. Yields above 5% appear in green.
Moat
Economic Moat

An economic moat is a durable competitive advantage that protects a company from competitors — like the moat around a castle. A wide moat company can maintain high profits for decades because it is very difficult for rivals to take its customers.

Wide Durable 10+ year advantage
Narrow Some advantage, vulnerable within 10 years
None Easily competed away, commodity business
Examples from our screener

Wide: Safaricom (M-Pesa network effects), Equity Bank (agent banking network), Capitec (tech-driven cost advantage). Narrow: East African Breweries (brand strength but facing competition). None: Commodity miners, airlines, basic construction firms.

Why it matters: A wide moat company is more likely to still be profitable in 10 years. Without a moat, today's profits can evaporate when a competitor enters or commodity prices fall.
Moat on The African Oracle is scored from sector type, net margin level, and EPS growth trajectory. Wide moat is one of the strongest buy signal inputs.

Fin Strength
Financial Strength

Financial strength measures how healthy a company's balance sheet is — can it pay its debts, does it generate consistent cash, and is it profitable enough to survive a rough patch?

Strong Low debt, high margins, consistently profitable
Adequate Moderate debt, mostly profitable
Weak High debt, thin margins, or losses
How we score it

For non-banks: a Debt/Equity below 0.5 combined with a net margin above 8% scores Strong. For banks (which naturally carry high leverage): we use adjusted thresholds since bank D/E of 8–10x is normal and not dangerous.

Why it matters: A financially weak company may be forced to cut dividends, dilute shareholders by issuing new shares, or even go bankrupt during an economic downturn. Strong companies can survive and often emerge stronger.
Predictability
Earnings Predictability

Predictability measures how consistent and reliable a company's earnings have been over the past 5 years. A company that grows steadily is far easier to value than one with wild swings.

High Positive, growing EPS for 4+ of 5 years
Medium Mostly positive with some volatility
Low Frequent losses or wildly erratic earnings
High vs Low examples

High predictability: Co-operative Bank of Kenya — grew EPS from KES 1.71 to KES 5.04 over 5 years without a single year of loss. Low predictability: Thungela Resources (TGA) — swung from profit to a massive loss of -KES 54.64 EPS due to commodity price volatility.

Why it matters: High predictability companies are easier to hold long-term without anxiety. Low predictability companies may offer big rewards but require higher risk tolerance and closer monitoring.
Margins
Net Profit Margin

Net profit margin is the percentage of revenue a company keeps as profit after paying all costs — salaries, materials, interest, taxes and everything else. It tells you how efficiently a business converts sales into actual money.

Net Margin = Net Profit ÷ Total Revenue × 100
Real example — Absa Bank Kenya (ABSA)

Absa Kenya earned KES 22.9 billion in net profit on KES 61.4 billion of total operating income in FY2025. Net margin = 22.9 ÷ 61.4 = 37.3%. This is exceptionally high — for every KES 100 of income, Absa kept KES 37.30 as profit.

What to look for: Margin improvement over time (initial to final) shows a company becoming more efficient. Declining margins suggest cost pressures or competition eroding pricing power. Sector context matters — banks and insurers have very different margin profiles to retailers.
We show both Initial Margin (FY2020) and Final Margin (FY2024) as a simple before-and-after view of profitability improvement.

D/E
Debt-to-Equity Ratio

Debt/Equity compares how much the company owes (debt) to what shareholders own (equity). It is a measure of financial leverage — how much borrowed money is being used to run the business.

D/E = Total Debt ÷ Total Shareholder Equity
Important context for banks

For regular companies, a D/E below 0.5 is low risk, while above 1.5 raises concern. But banks are different — they naturally borrow (deposits) to lend. A D/E of 5–8x is completely normal for a healthy bank like Equity or KCB. On The African Oracle, we apply separate thresholds for banking tickers.

Why it matters: High debt companies are more vulnerable when interest rates rise or revenue falls. A low D/E company has more financial flexibility and is less likely to need a rights issue (selling new shares) during tough times.
Intrinsic Value
Estimated Fair Value

Intrinsic value is an estimate of what a share is truly worth based on its earnings, as opposed to what the market is currently trading it for. The gap between intrinsic value and current price is called the margin of safety.

Intrinsic Value = EPS × P/E Ratio
Real example — KCB Group (KCB)

KCB's EPS is KES 16.67 and its P/E is 4.1. Intrinsic value = 16.67 × 4.1 = KES 68.3. If the current market price is KES 67.75, the stock is trading close to its intrinsic value. If price fell to KES 40, there would be a substantial margin of safety of ~40%.

Important caveat: This is a simplified Graham-style valuation. Real intrinsic value calculations are more complex and involve future earnings projections and discount rates. Our figure is a starting point for comparison, not a precise target price.
On each stock page we show intrinsic value alongside a margin of safety indicator — green if the stock appears undervalued, red if it appears overvalued.
